Active Looking

A Pedagogical Approach to Multimodal Compositions

Active Looking

looking, adj

Pronunciation: ‘lʊkɪŋ

look v. + ing suffix (gerund) – often hyphenated in combination with another adjective.

ex. That is a handsome-looking horse.

*Inconsequential to the term*

looking, n.

Pronunciation: ‘lʊkɪŋ

look v. + ing suffix  – usually paired with a copular (is looking)

– Lanham pairs ‘looking’ with AT and THROUGH in his article Electronic Word

1. the progressive action of ‘to look’ (v.)

ex. “We are always looking first AT it then THROUGH it, and this oscillation creates a different implied ideal of decorum; both stylistic and behavioral” (Lanham 267).

a. Implies an active participation with an object or idea → Lanham uses various texts to illustrate his point.

b. If we agree with Kathleen Blake Yancey, that the page has an interface – read “line by line, left to right… page one before page two” (317), then it is not a leap to Collin Gifford Brooke’s book Lingua Fracta in which Brooke frames the computer desktop in Lanham’s terms asking “Is the desktop something we look at or through?” Brooke follows this question with an interesting answer: “It depends where we look from” (134).

active, adj.

Pronunciation: U.S. ‘æktɪv

1. of a discipline: practical, applied; empirical, experimental – Oxford English Dictionary has marked this definition as obscure, but I argue that it is relevant in terms of a pedagogical practice.

2. capable of acting upon something; originating or communicating action; voluntary. Opposed to passive.

ex. Aunt Ida had an active lifestyle as a horseback rider and yoga instructor until she died at 92.

ex. “the effort put forth by students seemed to be one aimed at pleasing the instructor rather than one illustrative of active engagement with their classmates or the texts” (Hawisher and Selfe 61).

Active Looking, n.

Pronunciation: ‘æktɪv ‘lʊkɪŋ

1. A process of seeing with specific intent to then produce

a. From the 2004 Wysocki/Jasken: “How we construct understandings through any of our senses is very often mediated through words quietly derived from the process of seeing” (31).

NOTE how ‘look’ is used in the following examples:

I. [2002] “You have also looked at maps to note how a map can convey a particular argument of idea” (George 33).

II. [2003] “Whenever students look at artifacts… we can begin by teaching them to ‘read’ critically assumptions about gender, age, nationality, or other identity categories” (Hocks 630).

III. [2006] “Looking closely at a particular program makes one more likely to question its inevitability, to analyze particular features and functions of the tool, and to avoid seeing it is merely a tool or neutral instrument” (Sorapure 427).

Active Looking

2. implies looking to uncover/make transparent composite culture of composition, with intent to produce new materials in a new media framework.

3. It is a pedagogical system of inquiry designed to expose all layers of composition across modes and literacies.

4. I propose that a deployment of ‘Active Looking’ in a classroom setting will overtly point student attention toward a task of de-layering composited multimodal compositions and including multiple modes of meaning.

5. It is a specific branch of critical analysis pointing students toward a ‘best’ means of production.

a. If Daniel Anderson is correct and the “composing process can be partially understood by looking at the arrangement of the elements” (54), then it is my hope that Active Looking as a pedagogical strategy can help us to understand processes shared between modes, and modalities demanding specific skill-sets.
