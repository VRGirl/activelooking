A Pedagogical Approach to Multimodal Compositions

Emerging Practices

In her 2004 article, “Encouraging Emergent Moments: The Personal, Critical and Rhetorical in the Writing Classroom,” Rochelle Harris asks the following question: “Can critical work emerge from students’ processes of composing texts on topics of interest and importance to them?” (402). Many teachers believe that students need parameters and strict assignments with guidelines, goals, and rubrics in order to get desired results. But Harris finds that students will often “claim their own agency and become active participants in critiquing” (402), without strict facilitation by the teacher.

In a push-back against standardized testing, a team of scholars from several disciplines, looked at how positive learning outcomes can emerge without a set system of hierarchy (Dalke, Cassidy, Groebstein and Blank). In defining ‘emergence’ as “a pragmatic perspective on puzzle solving that assumes no conductor (no one anticipating future outcomes)” (113), Dalke et. al. explain that emerging practices can be organic in nature, at times expanding its scope to include the group level, encouraging both individual discovery as well as community interaction so that both individual and group produce results that indicate that learning outcomes are being fulfilled.

If we think of emerging practices as a part of the social process of learning, facilitating a studio arts environment, as Daniel Anderson calls it, could allow students to learn to compose with new media both as individual learners and as members of a production community. In this way, students and instructors learn as the go along, producing a community of learners who share as they experiment.
