Active Looking

A Pedagogical Approach to Multimodal Compositions

Introduction

3December , 2012 by valerievisual

Look.
Do you know what you are looking at?
Are you aware of what you are not looking at?
Watch.
Watch this.


Are you aware of what you are watching?
What’s the difference between watching and looking?
Does one exist?
When we watch, we consume. Watching is a passive construction of our time.
Does meaning making happen even though it is not the sole occupation of passive consumption?
When we look, is making meaning the primary focus? How do we know? Can we measure meaning making?


We would definitely not say these children are watching, but looking, as they learn.
As an instructor of First Year Composition in the university system,
I ask my students to watch several videos a semester.
It’s rare that I have time to show a video more than once,
and I have never asked for anything other than a discussion,
or an analysis of its meaning.
But what happens when instructors ask students to look closely AT and THROUGH a multimodal text?
As Richard Lanham discusses in his text Electronic Word,
“The textual surface has become permanently bistable.
We are always looking first AT it then THROUGH it,
and this oscillation creates a different implied ideal of decorum;
both stylistic and behavioral” (267).
Could an actively close look at multimodal texts, such as video,
lead to an exposure of the composited elements involved in all modes of composition?
This non-linear,interactive essay presents a three-part literature review to reveal
a need for a new pedagogical term I call Active Looking, which is a form of
critical analysis meant specifically for the multimodal classroom,
based around emerging practices in college composition.
