Active Looking

A Pedagogical Approach to Multimodal Compositions

Composite

According to Megan Fulwiler and Kim Middleton in “After Digital Storytelling: Video Composing in the New Media Age,” compositing is “the new literacy of ‘layering’ required by the standard operations in digital video composition” (43).

Composites exist across all modes of writing we ask our students to participate in. It is a part of various modes of writing, and is not limited to multimodality.

Sound:

Below is a screen shot of an Audacity recording I did in a café. I recorded the background noise in one room and laid the track over the sound I recorded in another room.

Cafe Sounds – Recorded by Valerie Robin

Audacity is what Daniel Anderson calls a “low-bridge technology” because it is free-downloadable software that requires no other equipment besides a computer with a microphone. Here, you can see two distinct sound tracks layered on top of one another. From this raw recording, I can cut bits out, add new sounds, adjust volume, and perform lots of other activities to make a composited sound track for whatever purpose I may wish.

Below is a screen shot from Movie Maker Live, a free video editing software that comes with most PC’s.

Shot from ‘watch‘ featured in this essay-blog

Megan Fulwiler and Kim Middleton call video “the paradigmatic example of a multimodal text with the potential for wide-ranging cultural, aesthetic and social implications” (40). Here we can see the video itself, it has not begun playing. To the right, we can see that a sound track has been layered on top of the image track. The image track has some kind of lead-in transition and below, in pink, we can see that text has been layered on top of the video, as indicated by the ‘A.’ Above the video, we can see that the menu, like in Audacity, offers many options for adding and changing the composited layers.

In this snip from my draft, you can see plain text, like we might see in any traditional paper, but there is also a static image layered on top of a hard line I created to indicate a break in pages. Next to the static image is a text box with the URL where I took the common use image. Below that, the text continues. The program has recognized my layers and has corrected the layout of the plain text accordingly so that it fits beside the photograph and below the moveable text box.

Active Looking then, exposes the layers in all modes → encouraging the transparency of all of what Daniel Anderson calls “construction sites” where we create and build compositions (42). Anderson uses a metaphor to apply the composite concept to the composition classroom when he says that “New media is too often an additive, something layered over existing pedagogies and analytical modes. An alternative model, which begins with the notion of production as the central activity of the classroom, is the studio arts” (57). In an art studio, compositing is part of an artistic composition. Art students engage in what I am calling ‘Active Looking’ every time they enter the studio. Perhaps Anderson is not wrong here in encouraging us to think of the classroom as a studio.
