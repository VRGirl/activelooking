Critical Analysis

In 1930, William Empson talked about 7 ambiguities of poetic analysis. He began with metaphor, and covered connection through context, and several other concepts that instructed the critical reader in making meaning through what we would now call critical reading.

In 1939, I.A. Richards published a book called Practical Criticism in which he describes a means of meaning making through close reading.

These ideas brought on the New Criticism movement in the U.S. which gained heightened popularity in the second half of the century with John Crowe Ransom’s book The New Criticism. Though New Criticism was a short-lived movement, it influences the way we think about analysis and the making of meaning.

Over the last several weeks, with the help of a research librarian, I looked for a comprehensive definition of ‘critical analysis’ that I could insert here. It turns out though, that chasing down a definition of ‘critical analysis’ is a bit like chasing down a definition of ‘rhetoric.’ However, where rhetoric returns dozens of varying definitions, critical analysis returns almost none.

Here, we can see that critical analysis  is a more modern version of poetic criticism. Composition instructors use critical analysis as a pedagogical tool to help students understand concepts like rhetorical analysis, compositing, and gleaning critical cultural meanings from texts of various modes.

Ransom tells us that critical writing is “in depth and precision at once” (x), and so, for my purposes, is critical analysis in general.
